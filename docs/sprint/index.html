1.1 Problem Statement

Australian solar installers are losing revenue and wasting hours every week because the tools they use for quoting, CRM, scheduling, project management, and STC compliance do not work together. They pay for three to five separate subscriptions that create manual handoffs, data re-entry, and gaps where sales fall through. Smaller operators become overwhelmed during busy periods and lose track of active leads. Schedulers manage complexity across weather delays, product substitutions, warranty recalls, and subcontractor availability without any purpose-built workflow to support them. There is no single platform built end-to-end for the Australian solar install workflow.

Quantified Evidence (pre-interview)
  • VALIDATED Rodney, Envirogroup — data re-entry for the same information measured at 13 separate entry points. Reduced to 3 with process work alone. Could have reached 1 with middleware.
  • VALIDATED Rodney, Envirogroup — quote turnaround for solar hot water reduced from 30+ minutes average to 2 minutes by redesigning the quoting process.
  • PARTIAL Daniel Lake, Sure Solar — Pylon provides no subcontractor login capability; an entire class of business operator is structurally underserved by the market leader.

E1.1 — Manual Lead Entry
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to manually enter a new lead with contact details, source, and initial notes, so that no inbound enquiry is lost regardless of channel.
  • AC: Required fields — name, phone or email (one required). Optional — source, address, system size interest, notes. Lead appears in the pipeline immediately on save. Source dropdown includes: Referral, Solarquotes, Solar Choice, Why Solar, Walk-in, Phone, Email, Other. Contact origin field (required): Inbound (customer contacted us) or Outbound (we contacted the customer — door-knock, cold call, event approach). Default is Inbound. Where the source is deterministic, contact origin is auto-set: Solarquotes, Solar Choice, Why Solar, Email, Walk-in, Referral → Inbound. Salesperson can override. Contact origin is stored on the lead record, displayed on the customer file, and is available to the DocuSign contract flow (E3.12) for audit trail. Legal research confirmed ACL cooling-off applies to virtually all residential in-home solar contracts regardless of Inbound vs Outbound — see E3.12 for the verified mechanism. When contact origin is Outbound, a red banner is shown on the lead record and the job record: "ACL cooling-off applies — 10-business-day deposit hold required." When contact origin is Inbound, a softer amber banner is shown: "ACL cooling-off likely applies — deposit hold is still enforced at contract stage (E3.12)." The Inbound/Outbound field is retained as a compliance audit trail; the actual cooling-off hold is controlled at contract stage regardless of this flag.
E1.2 — Email Forwarding Inbox
  • Story: As a business owner, I want a dedicated email address I can forward customer enquiries to, so that emails automatically create leads in the CRM with the enquiry text captured.
  • AC: Each business gets a unique inbound address (e.g. leads+{slug}@currentflow.com.au). Forwarded email creates a lead with sender name, email, and body as the initial note. Duplicate detection by email address — existing contacts get a new activity note, not a new lead. Attachments stored against the lead.
E1.3 — Interactive Booking Widget
  • Story: As a website visitor, I want to book a quote call or site visit online, so that I can progress without waiting for a business to call me back.
  • AC: Embeddable widget (iframe or script tag). Three entry types: Book a call, Book a site visit, General enquiry. Form defaults to three fields — Name, Phone, Email — consistent with the AU solar installer conversion standard (reducing from four to three fields yields approximately 50% completion rate improvement per HubSpot research, confirmed by Creative Nurds independent analysis of 12+ AU solar installer websites). Additional fields (system size interest, property address) optional and toggled per business. Lead temperature check questions shown after type selection (configurable per business — 3 to 5 questions max). Available time slots pulled from the business calendar. Confirmation email sent to customer immediately on submission. Lead created in CRM automatically.
E1.4 — Auto-Acknowledgment Email
  • Story: As a customer, I want to receive an automatic acknowledgment when I submit an enquiry, so that I know someone will contact me and approximately when.
  • AC: Email sent within 60 seconds of lead creation (any channel). Expected response time shown — default is the business's configured SLA target, with option to show the rolling 30-day average actual callback time instead. Email template fully customisable per business. Tone matches business branding.
E1.5 — SLA Alerting
  • Story: As a business owner, I want an alert if a new lead has not been contacted before the SLA window closes, so that no customer slips through without contact.
  • AC: SLA target configurable per business (default 4 hours, business hours only). Alert sent to assigned salesperson and owner by push notification and email. Alert escalates after a second interval if still uncontacted. Lead flagged visually in the pipeline. SLA clock pauses outside business hours (configurable).
E1.6 — PWA Call Integration
  • Story: As a salesperson on my phone, I want to tap a contact's number in CurrentFlow and have the call logged automatically, so that I do not have to manually record every outbound call.
  • AC: Click-to-call via PWA on Android and iOS (tel: protocol). Call attempt logged as an activity against the lead with timestamp, salesperson, and outcome (answered, voicemail, no answer — salesperson selects post-call). Optional: record and transcribe via AI (salesperson opts in per call; customer notified per jurisdiction requirements). Transcription stored against lead; summary surfaced in activity feed.
E1.7 — Salesperson Notes
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to add timestamped notes to a lead immediately after any contact, so that the full interaction history is visible to anyone handling the account.
  • AC: Rich text notes with @mention support for other team members. Notes feed the activity timeline. Notes visible on mobile in the same format as desktop. Voice-to-text input supported on mobile (device native).
E1.8 — Calendar Integration and Auto Follow-Up Email
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to book an appointment directly from a lead and have a confirmation email sent to the customer automatically, so that booking takes under 30 seconds and the customer always gets a confirmation.
  • AC: Integration with Google Calendar and Outlook Calendar (OAuth). Available slots pulled from the connected calendar. Appointment type determines which email template fires (quote call, site visit, follow-up call, proposal review). Customer receives calendar invite. Appointment appears on the salesperson's calendar and on the lead timeline. Reschedule and cancel flows handled within CurrentFlow.
E1.9 — Lead Temperature Gauge
  • Story: As a sales manager, I want each lead to display a temperature score driven by recency of contact and interaction depth, so that the team always works the hottest leads first.
  • AC: Temperature calculated from: days since last contact, number of interactions, whether a site visit or proposal has been sent, customer responsiveness (replies vs no-replies). Displayed as a colour-coded indicator (hot / warm / cold) on the pipeline card. Configurable weighting per business (stretch). Leads inactive beyond a configurable window auto-move to a "needs attention" view.
E1.10 — Win/loss capture
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to record a structured reason when I mark a lead as Lost, so that the business can identify the most common objections and adjust its approach over time.

Default reason options (configurable per business): Price too high, Chose competitor (name optional), Decided not to proceed, No response after follow-up, Bought elsewhere, Timeline too long, Finance declined, Other. Optional free-text note for context. Reason is displayed permanently on the lead record and cannot be edited after save — a new note can be added for corrections but the original reason is retained. Loss reason feeds the pipeline reporting (E4) and the conversion report (E4.8) so trends are visible per source, per salesperson, and over time.
E1.11 — Quote follow-up automation
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want an alert when a sent quote has not been opened or responded to within a configurable window, so that I never let a warm prospect go cold because I forgot to follow up.

Alert types: in-app notification and optional email reminder to the salesperson — not to the customer. Alert shows customer name, quote value, days since sent, and a one-click link to the quote. If the salesperson logs a call, adds a note, or sends a message against the lead, the follow-up clock resets for another N days. Alert repeats up to a configurable maximum (default 3 cycles) then stops and flags the quote as "stale" for owner visibility. Salesperson can manually dismiss a reminder from the notification.
E1.12 — Re-engagement workflow for cold leads

  • AC: Re-engagement trigger fires when a lead has had no logged activity for a configurable number of days (default 45 days). A "Stale leads" view in the pipeline shows all leads past this threshold, sorted by last-contact date. Owner or salesperson can launch a re-engagement sequence: up to 3 configurable touches at configurable intervals (e.g. email day 1, call attempt day 3, final email day 7). Each touch is logged in the activity timeline as a system-prompted action. At the end of the sequence the salesperson must disposition the lead: Re-activated (returns to active pipeline) or Archive (captures a loss reason per E1.10 and removes from pipeline while retaining in reporting). A monthly digest sent to the owner shows how many stale leads were re-engaged, how many converted, and how many were archived.
9a.3 Epic E12 — Security and Data Compliance (seeded)

Security is not a late-stage concern. These stories must be satisfied by architecture decisions in Stage 9c and enforced from the first line of code. Non-compliance with the Privacy Act or the NDB scheme carries legal and reputational risk that cannot be patched after launch.

E12.1 — Multi-Tenant Data Isolation
  • Story: As a business owner, I want certainty that my customer and job data is inaccessible to any other business on the platform, so that a data breach in another tenant cannot expose my data.
  • AC: Every data query is scoped to the authenticated tenant's ID at the ORM or query layer — never filtered in application logic post-fetch. Penetration test (prior to launch) must confirm no cross-tenant data leakage via any API endpoint, direct object reference, or error message. Architect decision recorded in ADR-05.
E12.2 — Role-Scoped Access Enforcement
  • Story: As a subcontractor, I can only see jobs assigned to me or available for claiming — I cannot access the CRM, pricing, other contractors' details, or any business financials, even by guessing URLs.
  • AC: All API endpoints enforce RBAC server-side. Subcontractor tokens cannot retrieve contact records, quote values, or other jobs. Automated test suite includes a role-crossing test for every protected endpoint. No client-side-only access control anywhere in the codebase.
E12.3 — Privacy Act and APP Compliance
  • Story: As a customer whose data is held by a solar business using CurrentFlow, I want my personal information handled in accordance with the Australian Privacy Principles, so that I have rights over how it is used, stored, and disclosed.
  • AC: Privacy Policy published and linked from the customer-facing booking widget and any customer-facing portal. Data collected is limited to what is necessary for the solar installation workflow (APP 3 — collection of solicited personal information). Customers can request access to their data and request deletion (APP 12/13). Retention policy defined and enforced — data not kept beyond the period needed for legal and tax obligations. Legal review of Privacy Policy before launch.
E12.4 — Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) Readiness
  • Story: As the operator of CurrentFlow, I want an documented process for detecting, assessing, and notifying affected parties in the event of a data breach, so that we meet the 30-day NDB scheme notification requirement under the Privacy Act.
  • AC: Incident response runbook written and accessible to the founding team before launch. Audit logs (see E12.6) provide the evidence trail needed to assess scope of a breach. OAIC notification process documented. Notification template for affected customers and businesses drafted. Annual review scheduled.
E12.5 — Call Recording Consent
  • Story: As a salesperson using call recording, I want the system to handle consent notification correctly for my jurisdiction, so that recordings are legally obtained.
  • AC: In all Australian states, at least one party must consent to recording — in practice this means the salesperson (who opts in to recording) satisfies this. However, some businesses may want both-party consent. The system must display a clear opt-in prompt to the salesperson before each recorded call. A consent configuration option (one-party / two-party) must be available in business settings. If two-party is selected, the system must prompt the salesperson to notify the customer verbally before recording begins — this is not automated (the system cannot intercept a call). Legal note documented in Settings UI.
E12.6 — Audit Logging
  • Story: As a business owner, I want a tamper-evident log of who accessed or changed sensitive records, so that I can investigate any suspicious activity and demonstrate compliance if audited.
  • AC: Audit log captures: actor (user ID + role), action (read/create/update/delete), resource (entity type + ID), timestamp, IP address. Logged for: all contact record access, all quote creation/modification, all STC lodgement actions, all user permission changes, all API key access. Log entries are append-only — no update or delete permitted. Log retained for minimum 7 years (tax record obligation). Log accessible to Business Owner role only, not to standard users.
E12.7 — Encryption and Credential Security
  • Story: As the platform operator, I want all data encrypted in transit and at rest, and all third-party API credentials stored securely, so that a server compromise does not expose customer data or integration credentials in plaintext.
  • AC: TLS 1.2 minimum on all endpoints (TLS 1.3 preferred). Database encryption at rest (managed by hosting provider — document in ADR-14). API keys for Formbay, Nearmap, Xero stored in a secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, Doppler, or equivalent) — never in environment variable files committed to version control. Call recording audio files encrypted at rest in object storage. Passwords hashed with bcrypt or Argon2 — no MD5, no SHA-1, no plaintext.
E12.8 — Session Management
  • Story: As a business owner, I want sessions to expire after inactivity and for staff to be forcibly logged out when their account is deactivated, so that ex-employees cannot retain access after leaving.
  • AC: Session timeout: 8 hours of inactivity for desktop, 30 days for mobile (with remember-me). Deactivating a user account immediately invalidates all active sessions for that user. 2FA via TOTP is mandatory for Owner and Admin roles at launch — these users are prompted to enable 2FA on first login and cannot dismiss the prompt permanently (see E12.9 for the full 2FA flow). 2FA is optional for Salesperson and Installer roles, with an optional business-wide enforcement setting the Owner can enable. Session tokens are rotated on privilege escalation (e.g. accessing audit logs).
E12.10 — Offline data encryption on device
  • Story: As the platform operator, I want any customer data cached on an installer's device for offline use to be encrypted so that a lost or stolen phone cannot expose job or customer information by reading the device's local files.
  • AC: Offline-capable features (job view, compliance photo capture, completion checklist — see E6, E8, E13) store only the minimum data required for that day's assigned jobs. No full customer database is cached on device. Cached data is encrypted using the Web Crypto API (AES-GCM 256-bit) in the browser's origin-partitioned storage (IndexedDB). The encryption key is derived from the user's server-issued session token at login time and held only in memory — it is never written to disk or localStorage. On logout, session expiry, or account deactivation, the in-memory key is discarded and all locally cached data becomes unreadable without re-authentication. The PWA does not rely on the device OS's file-level encryption as a substitute — it encrypts at the application layer regardless of device settings. Data minimisation: cached records include job address, customer first name, system spec, and compliance checklist templates only — no financial data, no full contact history, no other customers' records. Photos captured offline are held encrypted in the origin's storage and uploaded to the server as soon as connectivity returns, then purged from local storage on confirmed upload. A configurable idle timeout (default 4 hours, Owner-adjustable) logs the installer out of the PWA and clears the in-memory key even without a manual logout.
E12.9 — Two-factor authentication (TOTP)
  • Story: As a business owner, I want to enable two-factor authentication for my account using an authenticator app, so that my business data is protected even if my password is compromised.
  • AC: 2FA method: TOTP only (RFC 6238 — compatible with Google Authenticator, Authy, 1Password, etc.). No SMS delivery — TOTP is the sole second factor. Setup flow: user scans a QR code in their profile security settings, enters a 6-digit code to verify, and is shown 10 single-use backup codes to store offline. Backup codes can be regenerated (invalidates previous set) and are displayed once only. 2FA is mandatory for Owner and Admin roles — these users are prompted to enable 2FA on first login and cannot dismiss the prompt permanently. 2FA is optional for Salesperson and Installer roles, with an optional business-wide enforcement setting (Owner can require all roles to enable 2FA). When 2FA is enabled, the login flow prompts for the 6-digit code after password entry. Failed code attempts are rate-limited: 5 attempts, then a 10-minute lockout with an email notification to the account holder. Backup codes are accepted in place of the TOTP code. An Owner can remove 2FA from a user account (e.g. if they lose their device) via the user directory — this action is logged and triggers a notification to the affected user.
E12.11 — Owner-visible audit log
  • Story: As a business owner, I want to view a plain-English activity log showing who changed what and when across the system, so that I can investigate disputes, verify staff actions, and satisfy myself that sensitive records are only being accessed by the right people.
  • AC: Audit log screen is accessible to Owner role only at Settings → Audit Log. The log is a read-only, reverse-chronological feed of all recorded events (sourced from E12.6 internal logging — no additional data capture required). Each entry displays: timestamp, acting user name and role, action description in plain English (e.g. "Jane Smith changed quote Q-2041 total from $14,500 to $13,200"), and the record type and identifier affected. Events covered in v1: login and logout, failed login attempts, user account created / deactivated / role changed, quote created / price changed / sent / accepted / declined, job status changed, commission rate changed, payment recorded, customer record viewed (accessed), customer record edited, API key added or removed, system settings changed, 2FA enabled or removed on any account. Filtering: by date range (default last 30 days), by user, and by record type (Quotes, Jobs, Users, Settings, Payments). Search: free text across action description. Export: filtered log exportable as CSV. Log retention: 24 months accessible in-app; older entries archived and available on request. Log entries are immutable — they cannot be edited or deleted by any user including Owner. Pagination: 50 entries per page.
E12.12 — Australian data residency
  • Story: As a solar business owner, I want to know that all my business and customer data is stored on servers located within Australia, so that I can satisfy Australian Privacy Principles obligations and assure my customers their personal information is not sent offshore.
  • AC: All CurrentFlow production infrastructure (application servers, primary database, backups, object storage for documents and photos) is provisioned exclusively in an Australian cloud region (AWS ap-southeast-2 Sydney or Azure Australia East — never us-east-1, eu-west, or any non-AU region). No personal data or business data is replicated to a non-AU region for any purpose including analytics, logging, or disaster recovery. Third-party services that receive customer personal data (e.g. DocuSign, Xero, Twilio) are assessed against APP 8 cross-border disclosure requirements before integration and documented in the privacy policy. Data residency commitment is stated explicitly in the privacy policy and on the public pricing/trust page: "Your data is stored in Australia." Any future infrastructure change that would move data outside Australia requires a privacy impact assessment and owner notification before taking effect. Penetration testing and security audits reference the AU-region boundaries as scope. Data residency status is verifiable on request (e.g. Owner can request a written confirmation naming the cloud region).
E12.13 — Terms of Service and Privacy Policy acceptance on account creation
  • Story: As a new business owner signing up for CurrentFlow, I want to explicitly accept the Terms of Service and Privacy Policy before I can access the product, so that the contractual relationship is established from the first interaction.
  • AC: Before a new tenant account is activated, the signing-up business owner must accept the CurrentFlow Terms of Service and Privacy Policy. The system does not hard-block procurement but displays a prominent warning on the job record while the cooling-off countdown is active: "Cooling-off period active — ordering materials before this expires creates financial risk if the customer cancels." State note: Victoria bans door-to-door solar sales outright for Solar Homes Program and VEU program participants (Energy Legislation Amendment (Energy Fairness) Act 2021, effective 1 September 2021 and 1 August 2024 respectively); NSW bans doorknocking for ESS/PDRS scheme participants (August 2025). 9a.23 Epic E20 — Bill-Based System Sizing (seeded)

Sources: Gap audit Jun 14 2026. Sits at the start of the sales conversation — before formal design (E2) and before quoting (E3). A salesperson uses it during the initial consultation or site visit to derive a recommended system size and battery answer directly from the customer's electricity bill. The recommendation feeds E2 as a starting panel count and E3 as the system size basis. ADR required: bill parsing technology (third-party AU bill parser API vs purpose-built OCR vs guided manual entry only).

E20.1 — Electricity bill upload and parsing
  • Story: As a salesperson during a consultation, I want to upload or photograph the customer's electricity bill and have the system extract their usage data automatically, so that I can derive a system recommendation in under 2 minutes without asking the customer to find their online account or do mental arithmetic.
  • AC: Bill can be submitted three ways: photo taken with device camera (PWA camera API), PDF upload from device files, or image upload from camera roll. Parsed fields: retailer name, billing period (dates), total kWh consumed for the period, daily average kWh, current tariff rate (c/kWh) if present, controlled load (off-peak) usage if itemised, feed-in tariff rate if already a solar customer. Parser must handle the five highest-volume AU retailers (Origin, AGL, EnergyAustralia, Ergon/Energex, Synergy) at launch — coverage expanded over time. Parsed values shown to the salesperson for confirmation before proceeding — each field is editable in case OCR made an error. If the bill covers less than a full quarter, the system annualises the usage with a note. Technology decision: third-party AU electricity bill parsing API preferred over purpose-built OCR in v1 — document in ADR.
E20.2 — Manual usage entry fallback
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to enter usage data manually when the customer does not have their bill available or when the parser cannot read it, so that I can still generate a system recommendation without an upload.
  • AC: Manual entry screen presents: annual kWh (primary field — if known), or quarterly kWh across four quarters (entered separately to account for seasonal variation), or average daily kWh and a season adjustment toggle (high summer vs low winter). If only the customer's average bill amount is known (dollars, not kWh), the salesperson can enter the dollar amount and the system estimates kWh using an average tariff rate for the customer's postcode — result flagged as estimated and editable. Manual entry and parsed entry produce identical outputs downstream. The parser failure reason is logged for improving coverage (no customer data retained in the log — retailer name and failure type only).
E20.3 — Household usage profile
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to capture a quick household profile alongside the bill data, so that the system recommendation accounts for how and when the household uses electricity rather than just how much.
  • AC: Profile questions (configurable per business, presented after bill parsing — default set): Number of occupants, Hot water type (electric off-peak, electric continuous, gas, heat pump, solar hot water), Electric vehicle present (yes/no; if yes — overnight or daytime charging), Daytime occupancy (someone home during work hours yes/no — affects self-consumption). Each answer adjusts the self-consumption ratio used in E20.4 and E20.5. Answers stored against the lead record and visible in the job record. Profile can be updated later if circumstances change. Questions are framed conversationally for the salesperson to read aloud — not a form for the customer to complete themselves in v1.
E20.4 — System size recommendation
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want the system to recommend an optimal solar system size in kW based on the customer's usage and household profile, so that I have a justified starting point for the design conversation rather than guessing or defaulting to the same size for every customer.
  • AC: Recommendation calculated from: annual kWh, self-consumption ratio (from E20.3 profile), solar irradiance for the installation postcode (Bureau of Meteorology dataset or similar — performance ratio applied per E2.8 methodology), and a configurable offset target (default: size the system to offset 90% of annual usage; configurable per business between 70% and 120%). Result shown as: Recommended system size (kW), estimated annual solar generation (kWh), estimated self-consumed solar (kWh), estimated export to grid (kWh). If the customer's roof capacity from E2 (Nearmap canvas) is already available and is smaller than the recommended size, a warning is shown and the recommendation is capped to roof capacity with an explanation. Recommended size pre-populates the E2 design as the starting panel count (configurable — salesperson can accept or override).
E20.5 — Battery recommendation and payback
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want the system to tell me whether battery storage is financially justified for this customer and, if so, what size battery to recommend, so that I can give a confident battery answer without doing a manual calculation.
  • AC: Battery recommendation is calculated from: estimated export to grid (from E20.4), current feed-in tariff rate (from E20.1 parsed bill or manually entered), current peak import tariff rate (from E20.1 or manual), configurable battery cost (drawn from the component library in E5.1 for the recommended battery SKU). A battery is recommended when the calculated import saving exceeds the annualised battery cost within a configurable payback threshold (default: recommend if payback is under 8 years). Recommended battery capacity shown in kWh. Simple payback period shown in years. If battery is not recommended at current tariff rates, the system shows the break-even feed-in tariff rate at which it would become justified — useful for customers whose tariff may change. Battery recommendation links directly to the battery option in the multi-option proposal (E3.3) — salesperson can generate a "with battery" and "without battery" option from this screen in one action.
E20.6 — Projected bill savings and before/after comparison
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want to show the customer a simple before/after bill comparison with their recommended system, so that the financial case is immediately visible without the customer needing to interpret generation figures or do their own maths.
  • AC: Comparison shows: current estimated annual electricity spend (from bill data — kWh × tariff rate), projected annual spend with recommended solar system (import kWh × tariff rate, minus feed-in earnings), estimated annual saving ($), estimated saving as a percentage of current bill. If battery is included, a separate column shows the projected spend and saving with solar plus battery. Payback period shown for both system options. Figures clearly labelled as estimates based on current tariff rates and typical generation for the postcode — a one-line disclaimer is shown. Output can be sent to the customer as a PDF summary from the salesperson's device, or added to the quote proposal (E3) as a savings summary section. The figures used in this screen are the same figures that appear in the quote proposal — no discrepancy between what the salesperson showed and what the proposal says.
E20.7 — Link to design and quote
  • Story: As a salesperson, I want the system sizing recommendation to flow directly into the design tool and quote so that I do not re-enter the system size or have two sets of figures that can fall out of sync.
  • AC: From the E20 sizing screen, the salesperson can tap "Start design" — this opens E2 with the recommended system size (kW) pre-loaded as the target panel count, the job address pre-filled, and the usage data attached to the job record. The design can be adjusted freely; the E20 recommendation is a starting point, not a lock. From the E20 sizing screen, the salesperson can also tap "Create quote" to skip the formal Nearmap design step and create an E3 quote directly from the recommendation — useful for phone consultations where the salesperson cannot conduct a site design in real time. In both flows, the usage data, household profile, and sizing rationale are stored against the job record so any team member who picks up the account can see how the recommendation was derived. E20 data is visible in the job timeline.

9b.1 Design Principles
  • Mobile-first for field roles. Salesperson (on-site quoting, call logging, appointment booking) and installer (job brief, checklist, photo upload) must be fully functional on Android and iOS via PWA. Desktop is primary for office roles (scheduling, reporting, STC lodgement).

Schema: ComplianceTemplate (id, name, version, published_at, standard_references) → ComplianceSection (id, template_id, name, order) → ComplianceField (id, section_id, name, instruction_text, field_type [photo | serial_number | text | signature | checkbox], required, standard_clause, example_image_url). Jobs reference a specific template version at the time the compliance submission is started — standard updates do not change in-progress jobs. CurrentFlow maintains the standard templates and publishes updates when AS or CEC requirements change; businesses can extend with custom sections for their own additional requirements (e.g. DNO-specific inspections, council requirements). Decision options: (a) store templates as JSON in object storage with a version key, (b) store as normalised rows in a relational schema — normalised rows preferred for auditability and filtering by field. Research required: pull the current CEC installation requirements document and AS/NZS 5033:2021 table of contents to enumerate all required photo/evidence fields before building the first template. These standards must be verified against the current versions before sprint begin — they are not stable over time.
ADR-21Product catalogue — tenant-owned in v1, central repository in v2+OPENIn v1, each tenant manages their own product catalogue (E5.16). Product records are tenant-scoped: manufacturer, model, SKU, warranty terms, datasheet. A future central product repository — a separate database or API service — could serve verified manufacturer product data across all CurrentFlow tenants, so a business does not have to manually enter warranty terms for a Goodwe inverter that a hundred other businesses have already added. Design constraint for v1: the product record schema must include a source field (values: "tenant" or "central") and an external_id (nullable) for future sync with a central feed. No product record should be structured in a way that prevents it being replaced by or linked to a centrally-managed record later. Decision deferred: whether the central repository is a separate CurrentFlow-owned microservice, a licensed third-party component database (e.g. Pick Energy, Open PV), or a community-contributed dataset. This decision does not need to be made before v1 ships — the schema constraint above is sufficient to keep the door open. When the time comes, the central feed should be additive: tenants can still override or supplement central data with their own entries.
ADR-22Dual-role identity model — single user record vs linked recordsOPENA solo installer who subcontracts for other businesses (E7 contractor role) while also owning their own CurrentFlow tenant (E11) needs to navigate between both contexts in one session. Two candidate models: (a) Single user identity with multiple tenant memberships — one email, one login, tenant selector on login or in-app; simpler UX, requires that the auth layer stores role-per-tenant rather than a single role per user, and that data isolation is enforced at query time per tenant context. (b) Two separate user accounts — one for their own business, one as a contractor at other businesses — linked by a "same person" association; allows simpler role model in v1 but requires a separate login or account-linking flow that adds UX friction. Decision constraint: model (a) must be chosen if it is at all feasible, because model (b) is a user experience problem that Persona D (subcontractor-led operator) will hit immediately and that cannot be retrofitted without a schema migration. The auth ADR must be decided before the v1 auth layer is built even though the context-switching UI ships in v2 (E7.8, E21.2). Cross-context data isolation is a v1 security requirement regardless of when the switching UI ships — a contractor must never see CRM, pricing, or non-assigned job records from a business they are contracting for.
ADR-19Lead ingestion middleware — first-party, field-mapping layer, not ZapierOPENCurrentFlow builds and owns a first-party lead ingestion middleware layer. All lead sources — Solarquotes, Solar Choice, Why Solar, Meta Lead Ads, email parse (E1.2), inbound webhook, and any future source — route through this single layer before a CRM lead is created. No copy-paste from any source is ever required. Zapier, Make, and n8n are explicitly NOT the backend mechanism for lead ingest (they may be offered as a consumer-facing Zapier connector via E14.7, but the middleware is the authoritative path). Architecture: (1) Receiver — a normalised webhook endpoint per source, or an email parser for email-based sources. (2) Validator — schema and format checks on the raw payload. (3) Field mapper — maps source-specific field names to the canonical CurrentFlow LeadPayload schema (name, email, phone, address, system size interest, source, notes, raw payload archived). Mapping is pre-configured for standard integrations; UI-configurable for custom webhook sources (E14.9). (4) Deduplicator — phone/email match against existing contacts before creating a new lead. (5) CRM writer — creates the lead and fires the lead.created domain event. This design ensures: every lead has a source tag and an archived raw payload for debugging; the integration team adds a new source by writing one receiver + one field map, not a bespoke CRM-writer per source; no third-party platform sits in the critical path for a live lead.
9c.2 Known Stack Constraints (pre-decided context)
  • Xero API integration is mandatory — any backend must have a well-supported Xero SDK or REST client.
  • Formbay API is OAS 3.0 REST — any HTTP client works; no vendor lock-in from the integration itself.
  • Nearmap / Metromap APIs are tile-based — the frontend must render map tiles with a custom panel overlay layer, which points toward a library like Mapbox GL or Leaflet.
  • CEC component database is not a public API — a local cache of the approved product list (updated periodically) is required in the datastore.
  • Contractor job claiming requires pessimistic locking or a queue mechanism — "first claimed wins" on a concurrent row update is a race condition without explicit handling.
  • AU data residency is a commercial requirement — all customer data must stay in Australia (ap-southeast-2 or equivalent).
  • pnpm is the package manager for all JavaScript tooling. npm is banned.
  • Offline-first is a hard requirement for salesperson and installer mobile use cases — not a post-launch enhancement. The data sync and conflict resolution strategy must be decided in ADR-17 before the mobile architecture is built, not retrofitted.
  • SLD generation is a significant differentiator across the entire AU market (no competitor auto-generates it). The approach — rules-based generation, template engine, or code library — must be decided before E2 development begins.
  • Lead source integrations (Solarquotes, Solar Choice, Why Solar, Meta Lead Ads, custom webhooks) are business-critical at launch. All lead sources flow through a single first-party ingestion middleware — no copy-paste from any source, no Zapier dependency in the backend. ADR-19 is the architectural decision; E14.9 is the user-facing story. The middleware is a receiver, validator, field mapper, deduplicator, and CRM writer pipeline. Adding a new source means writing one receiver and one field map, not a bespoke integration per source.
